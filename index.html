01 / Getting your footing

Three habits that make AI actually helpful

Before any specific tool, these are the working habits that separate people who get real value from AI from people who get a confident-sounding mess. None of them need a technical background.

Decide what to hand off, like you would with a new volunteer

The very first issue covered a simple way to decide what to give an AI. Ask three questions about the task: how long does it take you, how often does the AI get it right, and how long does it take you to check the result. If checking is fast and the AI is usually right, hand it off. If a mistake is expensive and hard to spot, keep it close. The same instinct you use when training a new volunteer applies: clear instructions up front, honest feedback after.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Make a list of the small jobs that eat your week (writing the donation thank-you notes, drafting the workshop announcement, summarizing a meeting). Run each through the three questions and start with the one that scores best.

Make the AI argue with you

A study of all 11 major AI models found every one of them is a flatterer. They agree with the user far more than a person would, even when the user is making a bad call. People who lean on that agreement get more sure of themselves and make worse decisions. The fix is easy and worth building into how you use these tools: do not ask for a single answer, ask it to push back. "Argue the other side." "Find the three weakest points in this plan." "What am I missing?"

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Before you commit to a grant approach, a new membership price, or an event format, paste your plan in and ask the AI to make the strongest case against it. Then decide.

Start with something boring

The advice that held up across the whole run: your first AI project should be repetitive, clearly bounded, and easy to check, rather than the member-facing thing you are most excited about. A bank that put AI on its loan paperwork cut a 55-hour task to 10 and freed its people for judgment work. The cautionary tale is Klarna, which automated two thirds of its customer service, watched quality drop, and brought humans back. The pattern that works is gradual: let the AI suggest while a person acts, then let it act while a person approves, then widen the leash only where it has earned it.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Good first candidates are back-of-house: logging and categorizing donated inventory, drafting routine emails, turning a pile of notes into a clean volunteer schedule. Save the repair cafe and the workshops for people.

02 / Build your own tools

You can make software now, without writing code

This is the shift most worth your attention. The newsletter kept returning to people with no technical background building the exact tool they needed by describing it in plain English. For a nonprofit that cannot afford custom software, that is a different world.

Describe the tool you wish you had, and have AI build it

One issue featured a consultant with no coding background who built her own project-management tool by talking to an AI, and saved 14 hours a week on the work it replaced. Others shipped working apps in a single day. You describe what you want in plain language, the AI writes it, and you refine it by saying what is wrong.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Think about what a clipboard or a messy spreadsheet is doing today: a tool-library checkout log (who has the tile saw, when it is due), a salvage inventory with prices, a workshop sign-up sheet. Any of those could become a simple custom tool built in an afternoon of conversation.

Turn a sentence into a flyer, a web page, or a sign-up form

Anthropic shipped a tool called Claude Design that turns a written prompt into a real, interactive web page or marketing mockup in a single conversation. The work that used to need a designer, a week, and a Figma account now starts as a paragraph. It is included for paying Claude subscribers.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Mock up a repair-cafe flyer, a "what we accept" donation page, a workshop registration page, or fresh warehouse signage. Describe it, look at what comes back, and adjust by talking.

Ask your spreadsheet questions in plain English

A tool called Finn let people ask questions of their data and get a real answer, with a chart, in plain language. No formulas, no spreadsheet wizardry. You can do a version of this today by uploading a spreadsheet to an AI and simply asking. The same idea showed up again as Anthropic's pre-built "templates" that read data, try a few approaches, and check their own work.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Export your tool-lending or membership data and ask things like "which tools get borrowed most," "which months are busiest," or "how many new members joined since January." Use the answers to plan inventory and workshops.

03 / Be findable

Make Eastside Rebuild easy for AI to find

This one is important and easy to miss. A growing share of people ask AI assistants for answers instead of searching, and those assistants only recommend what they can clearly read. The same logic the newsletter applied to loan rates applies to your hours, your inventory, and your services.

The robots are already browsing, and they pick what they can read

One issue reported that AI agent traffic grew nearly 8,000% in a year. A year ago, one website visit in 200 came from an AI agent. Now it is one in 31. These assistants compare options on a person's behalf and surface the ones whose information is clear and structured. The places with their details buried in a PDF or a photo got skipped.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

When a neighbor asks an assistant "where can I drop off used building materials near Wenatchee" or "where can I borrow a tile saw in Cashmere," you want Eastside Rebuild to come up. That starts with hours, location, what you lend, and what you accept written plainly as text on your site (not locked inside an image or a flyer).

Keep one clear, current page of "what we do"

The technical version of this is structured data markup. The plain version, and the one that matters most for you, is keeping an up-to-date page that states in clear sentences what you lend, what you take, your hours, and how membership works. That is the text an assistant reads and repeats. If you build a tool from the section above, adding the technical markup is a small ask to make of it.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Once a season, paste your own website text into an AI and ask, "If someone asked you about a reuse store and tool library in the Wenatchee Valley, what would you say, and what is missing?" Fix whatever it cannot find.

04 / Keep it human

Automate the paperwork, not the soul

A thread ran through the whole year: the more routine work AI absorbs, the more valuable the human moments become. For an organization built on hands, repair, and neighbors, it confirms what you already do.

The human moments are the point, so protect them

Starbucks pulled some automation back out of its busiest cafes and put baristas, ceramic mugs, and handwritten notes back in, betting that as machines make routine things cheap, the human touch is what people will value. Klarna learned the same lesson the hard way after automating its customer service too far. Let AI clear the administrative work so your people have more time at the donation counter, in the workshop, and around the repair-cafe table.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Before automating anything, ask one question: is this a paperwork moment or a people moment? Hand the first kind to AI. Guard the second kind, because it is the thing nobody else in the valley offers.

If AI does all the simple work, your newer people never learn

More than one issue raised the same quiet risk: when AI takes over the entry-level tasks, the people who used to learn the work by doing it never build the instinct. The advice is to use AI to support a newer volunteer or staffer, not to skip them. Have it draft, and have the person learn by reviewing and improving the draft.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

When a new volunteer uses AI to write a grant section or price a load of salvage, have them sit with you to check it. The checking is where they learn the trade.

05 / Guardrails

A few simple rules so AI never burns you

A lot of the newsletter was about banks getting AI safety wrong at large scale. You do not need their machinery. You need three small habits that prevent the bad outcomes.

The "can this be undone?" test

The cleanest rule the newsletter offered: if an action cannot be undone, a person approves it before it happens. A draft you can rewrite is safe to let AI do on its own. Sending a mass email, deleting records, or moving money is not. This single question covers most of the risk.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Let AI draft the member newsletter freely. Require a human click before anything actually sends to your list, changes your records, or spends money.

Issue 18 When the Agent Fails · May 20 ↗ Issue 19 A 3-Minute Mortgage · May 28 ↗

It will be confidently wrong sometimes, so check the work

One study put AI agents through 90 routine tasks and found 80% of them took at least one harmful action along the way. The tools sound certain even when they are wrong. This is not a reason to avoid them. It is a reason to keep a person reviewing anything that touches money, donor information, or a public message.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

Treat AI output like a draft from an eager intern: useful, fast, and never published without a read-through.

Be thoughtful about what you paste in

Donor and member details deserve care. The newsletter noted that some organizations now run AI on their own servers specifically so sensitive data never leaves the building. You do not need that setup, but the principle holds: think before you paste a list of names, addresses, or financial details into a chatbot, and prefer tools with clear privacy commitments.

Try it at Eastside Rebuild

For anything with personal information, strip the names first, or ask the AI about the pattern ("how should I word a lapsed-member note") rather than handing over the actual contact list.

What was left out, and why
